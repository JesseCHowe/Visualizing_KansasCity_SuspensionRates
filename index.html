The Civil Rights Data Collection (CRDC) 2011 analysis of Elementary Suspension Gaps put Missouri in the spotlight two years ago, revealing that the state had the highest suspension rate of black elementary students in the nation at 14 percent and an elementary suspension gap of 12.5 black students suspended for every white student.

The CRDC data also showed that Missouri’s high suspension rates were weighted heavily by St. Louis and Kansas City, as both cities enroll the majority of Black Students in the state. The Kansas City metro enrolls 22 percent of black students in Missouri.

Kansas City Public Television has put together four visualizations on suspensions for the Kansas City metro, using data collected by the CRDC and the Missouri and Kansas Departments of Education, tracking out of school suspension (OSS) rates, the change in suspensions since 2011, and the reasons why students are suspended, based on current public data by the CRDC on racial disparities K-12.

Suspensions

2011 was an especially bad year for Kansas City Public Schools (KCPS), the district’s OSS rate doubled from 4.1 percent the year before to 8.3 percent, the highest rate the district has on record. The average of all districts stayed steady at around 1.7 percent. Nine districts lay above the region’s average including: Hickman Mills, Kansa City Public Schools, Turner, Bonner Springs, Kansas City Kansas, Center, Excelsior Springs, Grandview, and Fort Osage.

between 2008-2011 and 2012-2015. Hickman Mills, KCPS, KCK, Turner, Bonner springs, and Excelsior Springs have increased OSS rates over the past four years, while Center and Grandview have dropped significantly.

Steve Green, KCPT superintendent, attributed the high rates to large amounts of school closures (as reported the Kansas City Star in 2015) and that Kansas City “would show a decline with the next federal data collection”. According to, DESE data and 2013 CRDC data the district has kept lower suspension rates since 2011, but on average the district is still slightly higher than the years before 2011 by 0.6 percent.

When looking at suspension rate data, it becomes clear that the same districts that attributed to Missouri’s high elementary OSS rate in 2011, are districts that have large fluctuations in their suspension rates from year to year. Kansas City saw its lowest and highest suspension rates back to back. The trend also shows more urban school districts see higher fluctuations than suburban and rural districts, except for the largest outlier on the graph, Hickman Mills.

In 2015 the CRDC reported that Hickman Mills, had an even higher black suspension rate than KCPS, at 23.6 percent to KCPS’s 19.8 percent. 2011 wasn’t even Hickman Mills largest suspending year, beating KCPS’s 2011 high in 2014 at 8.6 percent.

Hickman Mills is well aware of their suspension rate, along with its provisional accreditation. New superintendent, Yolanda Cargile has already set out initiatives for replacing suspension rates with a restorative justice program, an approach that is also being applied to individual schools in the St. Louis area.

Racial Disparities

Not only has an overall suspension rate been an obstacle for Kansas City metro school districts, but a disparity between those who are suspended has also plagued the district. Disproportionate amounts of black students have been suspended compared to their white peers.

Between 2011 and 2013, 11 out of the 26 districts in Kansas City metro* have decreased the suspension gap, some as much as 7.1 percent. Others such as Oak Grove, Independence, and Raytown have seen increases in their suspension gap.

This data should be taken with a grain of salt, though it is true Raytown had a suspension rate of 21.9% in 2013, it had a suspension rate of 8% two years before. The CRDC has only collected suspension data biannually since 2009, and much like overall suspension rate from DESE, is susceptible to wild variations from year to year. Raytown has managed to decrease its number of overall suspensions in the metro, though those suspensions still being given out disproportionately affect black students .

Why students are suspended

The basis for suspensions is often left to individual districts, usually outlined in their student handbooks, but the common thread between all districts is that the majority of out of schools suspensions given are for nonviolent offenses.

These out of school suspensions can last anywhere from one day to 10days. The Washington Post reported in 2015 that students lost a cumulative 18 million days of instruction for out of school suspensions in the 2011-2012 school year”.

Between 2008 and 2015, 25 percent of suspensions were for violent offenses. The rest varied from alcohol, drugs, tobacco, and bringing a weapon to school, with a large amount being categorized under “other” in Missouri (Kansas does not have an “other category nor any additional categories).

How violent acts are categorized along with offenses that call for OSS are determined by each school board. In both KCPS and the Kansas City, Kansas school district, offenses are categorized I through IV. KCPS does not include possession of drugs or alcohol as a category III offense, while Kansas City, Kansas does, along with failure to remain seated on a bus and obscene gestures.

The difference in these policies can call for dramatic differences in data collection and categorization. Where Kansas City Public Schools had no more that 7 violent offenses in any given year since 2008 Kansas City, Kansas had 573 in 2012 alone.

Nationwide, as many as 95 percent of out-of-school suspensions are for nonviolent misbehavior--like being disruptive, acting disrespectfully, tardiness, profanity, and dress code violations.

Districts continue to look for ways to deescalate the suspension reputation that they have received in recent years, whether through addressing racial bias, basis for suspensions, and how schools approach discipline.

2016 numbers are already available on Missouri department of education site, where KCPS has reached a new record low of 2.2 percent and a record low 366 incidents, where 1,200 was the average. Hickman Mills 2016 numbers are also lower than ever before at a 2.7 percent suspension rate.

Although, just like in the past, it will be a few years until these can be confirmed as a positive trend or outlier, and even longer before improvement in racial disparities can be confidently confirmed.
